The Seward Park Canopy

Douglas Fir

Pseudotsuga menziesii (Pinacaea)

Seward Park is home to many Douglas Trees, members of the Pine family, which can grow to be extremely tall with some recorded at over 300 feet tall. Diamters commonly reach widths of eight feet but can easily grow to be more than ten feet wide. Most Douglas firs will grow for near 500 years though some have endured for over 1,000 years. Douglas Fir trees are native to North America.

Douglas Fir branches and seed-cones

Features

Douglas Fir trees feature soft small 'pine' needles about an inch long that completely cover the ends of the branches upon whoch they rest. The bark of the Douglas fir is smooth and gray on adolescent trees, but later turns a dark brown with large resin blisters and vertical fissures. As the tree ages, it sheds its lower branches. To reproduce, Douglas firs create seedcones (commonly known as pinecones) to spread their seeds.

Ecology

Douglas firs provide food and shelter to residents of their respective habitats. Many foraging animals such as the Squirrels that inhabit Seward Park eat the seeds of the Douglas Fir. Many of the bird species that reside in Seward park nest in the upper reaches of these trees. Many mosses and ivys likewise find homes on Douglas Firs, and the shade they provide under their canopies allows more shade tolerant plants to thrive in the understory.
